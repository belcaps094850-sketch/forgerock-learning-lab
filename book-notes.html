De-Positioning

Author: Todd Irwin  |  Topic: B2B Brand Strategy  |  Researched by: Sage üéØ  |  Date: February 2026

Overview

Traditional brand positioning ‚Äî trying to differentiate by claiming uniqueness ‚Äî no longer works in saturated markets. Instead, brands should de-position competitors by identifying real customer pain points that rivals can't or won't solve, then aligning the entire organization around eliminating that pain.

Differentiation alone is not enough. Brands win not by shouting louder, but by solving the one problem competitors leave unresolved ‚Äî making alternatives feel unnecessary.

The Six Key Principles

1. Abandon the Search for "White Space"

Every category is already occupied. Instead of inventing a new lane, redefine existing space by reframing how customers see current options. Identify a clear "enemy" ‚Äî not a competitor, but a problem or outdated way of doing things.

2. Find the "Hero Pain Point"

Not one of many problems ‚Äî the single most emotionally charged and practically urgent issue customers want solved. Focus creates power. Trying to address too many needs weakens impact.

3. Make the Customer the Hero

Customers don't wake up hoping to admire a brand. They wake up because something isn't working. Winning brands identify the obstacle and organize everything around removing it.

4. Study Competitors to Expose What They Can't Fix

Study rivals deeply ‚Äî not to copy, but to uncover what they cannot or will not fix. Every organization carries limitations. Those weaknesses are your strategic openings.

5. Own One Idea in the Customer's Mind

Brands that try to stand for too many things end up standing for nothing. One strong concept creates a mental shortcut ‚Äî easy to understand, easy to remember.

6. Integrate Across the Entire Organization

If the external promise doesn't match the internal experience, trust collapses. Every part of the business must reinforce the same strategic idea.

The De-Positioning Process

  1. Research & Observe ‚Äî Deep customer research + competitor analysis
  2. Identify the Hero Pain Point ‚Äî The single most urgent, unresolved customer frustration
  3. Map Competitor Weaknesses ‚Äî Where do rivals fail to solve this pain?
  4. Define Your One Idea ‚Äî The clear, ownable solution that makes alternatives feel outdated
  5. Align the Organization ‚Äî Every touchpoint reinforces the one idea
  6. Execute with Discipline ‚Äî Say no to anything that dilutes focus

The "Enemy" Framework

ElementDescription
The EnemyNot a competitor ‚Äî a problem, inefficiency, or outdated approach
The PainThe hero pain point customers feel most urgently

The ContrastHow your solution makes the old way feel inadequate

Case Studies

Apple ‚Äî The Second-Mover Masterclass

Didn't succeed by being first. Observed early players, let them introduce complexity and frustration, then entered with simplicity, integration, and design. De-positioned competitors as complicated and uninspiring.

Volvo ‚Äî Owning One Idea for Decades

Owned safety. Didn't try to be fastest, most luxurious, or most affordable. Every decision reinforced one concept. Discipline in owning one idea creates lasting brand equity.

What Is B2B Customer Experience?

B2B CX is how a business customer perceives your brand across every interaction ‚Äî from first research to onboarding, ongoing service, and renewal. Unlike B2C, it involves multiple stakeholders, longer sales cycles, higher stakes, and ongoing relationships.

B2B buyers are still humans. They bring the same expectations from their B2C experiences into professional purchasing. The gap between B2C ease and B2B friction is where trust breaks.

Buyer Anxiety Model

  1. Pre-purchase anxiety: "Am I choosing the right vendor? Will I look bad if this fails?"
  2. Purchase anxiety: "Is this contract fair? Am I missing something?"
  3. Post-purchase anxiety: "Did I make the right call? Is this working?"
CX must address all three phases. Most companies only focus on pre-purchase and ignore post-purchase ‚Äî where churn happens.

Six Key Themes Across B2B CX Research

1. Reduce Effort, Don't Delight

Loyalty comes from reducing effort, not exceeding expectations. Customers don't reward you for going above and beyond ‚Äî they punish you for making things hard.

2. Self-Service Is the New Default

65‚Äì81% of B2B buyers prefer to research and solve problems independently. Winning companies build better self-service tools, not bigger sales teams.

3. The Buying Committee Challenge

4. Post-Purchase Is Where the Money Is

In recurring revenue models, 80%+ of revenue comes after the initial sale. Yet most companies spend 80%+ of CX effort on acquisition.

5. Internal Alignment Is Non-Negotiable

Only 24% of service leaders have full-funnel visibility. The biggest CX failures aren't bad interactions ‚Äî they're disconnected interactions.

6. Emotional Experience Matters More Than You Think

Anxiety drives B2B decisions more than logic. Buyers choose vendors who make them feel safe and confident. Trust is the currency.

Digital Exhaustion: Simple Rules for Reclaiming Your Life

Author: Paul Leonardi  |  Publisher: Penguin Random House  |  Researched by: Alec ü¶û  |  Date: February 2026

Overview

Named a Best Management Book of 2025 by the Globe and Mail. Leonardi argues that unplugging isn't a realistic long-term solution ‚Äî if you want to keep your job, participate in society, and maintain relationships, you can't escape your devices. Instead, he maps out achievable, research-backed strategies for using technology in healthier ways.

"Unplugging is not a long-term solution for the stress caused by technology."

Key Principles

1. Delay Your Responses

Not everything needs an instant reply. The sense of urgency is mostly in our heads. Waiting longer to respond reduces pressure without real consequences.

2. Match the Tool to the Task

We default to the wrong communication channel constantly ‚Äî Slack when it should be email, email when it should be a call. Using the right tool for each task reduces friction and cognitive load.

3. Recognize Emotional Traps

FOMO, performative availability, and dopamine loops keep us locked into dysfunctional relationships with technology. Awareness is the first step to breaking free.

4. Be Intentional with Attention

Consciously allocate time and energy to devices instead of letting them dictate your schedule. Mental space is a finite resource ‚Äî protect it.

About the Author

Paul Leonardi, PhD is the Duca Family Professor of Technology Management at UC Santa Barbara. He consults for Google, Microsoft, YouTube, GM, McKinsey, and Fidelity ‚Äî helping organizations leverage technology while defeating digital exhaustion. He's a Harvard Business Review contributor and coauthor of The Digital Mindset.

Why This Matters

This isn't anti-tech. It's pragmatic tech ‚Äî written by someone embedded in the industry. The message: you can be productive and connected without feeling drained, if you're deliberate about how you engage.

Actionable Takeaways

Audit your response habits ‚Äî where are you replying instantly out of habit, not necessity?
Map your communication channels to tasks ‚Äî are you using the right tool for each type of work?
Identify your emotional triggers ‚Äî what keeps pulling you back to your phone?
Block intentional device-free time ‚Äî not to "unplug" but to create space for deeper work

Deming's Journey to Profound Knowledge

Author: John Willis (with Derek Lewis)  |  Publisher: IT Revolution Press (2023)  |  Researched by: Alec ü¶û  |  Date: February 2026

Overview

The story of how W. Edwards Deming developed his System of Profound Knowledge ‚Äî told through the historical events, people, and serendipities that shaped his thinking. Not a biography; it's the origin story of the ideas that became the foundation of Lean, Agile, and DevOps.

"I hope what you have heard here today will haunt you for the rest of your life. Then I have done my best." ‚Äî Dr. W.E. Deming

The System of Profound Knowledge

Deming's four interconnected domains for understanding and improving any system:

  1. Appreciation of Systems ‚Äî See the bigger picture, not just individual parts. The opposite of analytic thinking is systems thinking.
  2. Theory of Variation ‚Äî Understand how processes behave. Track variation to find defects early and distinguish signal from noise.
  3. Theory of Knowledge ‚Äî How do we know what we think we know? Rooted in pragmatism and the scientific method: observe ‚Üí hypothesize ‚Üí test.
  4. Theory of Psychology ‚Äî How do people behave in systems? Workers aren't cogs ‚Äî treat them as partners.

Key Concepts

The 94/6 Rule

Only 6% of problems are human error. 94% are system error. Since the system is management's responsibility, 94% of problems are caused by bad management. Stop blaming workers ‚Äî fix the system.

Common vs. Special Cause Variation

TypeDescriptionResponse
Common CauseInherent to the process, predictablePlan for it ‚Äî it's normal
Special CauseExternal, unpredictableInvestigate it ‚Äî something changed
"The root of all evil" = misidentifying which type of variation you're dealing with. Treating common cause as special (or vice versa) leads to worse outcomes.

Quality Is About Process, Not Product

"Quality is not so much about improving the product as it is about improving the process." Defects are a result of the process, not the workers. Less waste = doing more with less.

Management by Means (MBM)

If you don't know how you achieved your objective, you don't know if you can do it again. Focus on the method, not just the outcome.

Cooperation Over Competition

Even competitors working together benefits everyone ‚Äî especially customers. "Strive to be better than yourself ‚Äî that is true competition." Deming's real legacy: preaching cooperation over rivalry.

On People & Management

Ford saw workers as interchangeable cogs. Taylor saw them as machines to optimize. Both assumed workers don't want to work ‚Üí adversarial relationships.
The Hawthorne Works treated workers as partners ‚Äî and it worked. Japanese workers believed they were doing something that mattered.
Piecework incentivizes quantity over quality ‚Äî "man's lowest degradation."
Organizations need to be places where people can teach themselves.
"Our problems are different" is a common management disease ‚Äî the principles that help are universal.
To change a system, you need knowledge from outside that system. If the system had the knowledge, it would change itself.

Statistical Process Control (SPC)

SPC lets you observe variation and distinguish random patterns (stable, "under control") from non-random patterns (unstable, potential defects ahead).
Shewhart's method enabled management to see defects as a result of process instead of blaming workers.
SPC allows you to statistically predict defects before they occur.

  • Break down barriers between departments
